# **Quantitative Evaluation of Fiscal Uncertainty-Driven Yield Curve Strategies in Brazilian Interest Rate Markets**

## **Executive Summary**

The fixed-income architecture of emerging markets presents a highly complex confluence of macroeconomic volatility, shifting monetary policy regimes, and distinct term premium dynamics. Within the Brazilian financial ecosystem, the term structure of interest rates is primarily synthesized through One-Day Interbank Deposit (DI) Futures contracts traded on the B3 exchange.1 These instruments serve as the definitive benchmark for pricing sovereign risk, corporate credit, and derivative structures across the domestic economy, functioning as the primary conduit for monetary policy transmission.1 The present analysis delivers an exhaustive quantitative evaluation of a dynamic yield curve strategy engineered to trade the spread between the 1-year forward 1-year rate (1y1y) and the 3-year forward 3-year rate (3y3y) on the Brazilian DI curve.  
The core theoretical postulation underpinning the strategy posits that elevated fiscal uncertainty induces a systemic expansion of the term premium, thereby precipitating a bear steepening of the forward yield curve. Conversely, subdued fiscal uncertainty theoretically compresses the term premium, facilitating curve flattening and the systematic collection of carry.4 However, the empirical execution of this strategy across six developmental iterations reveals a profound macroeconomic paradox intrinsic to the Brazilian yield curve. When fiscal uncertainty rises, the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB) frequently intervenes with aggressive monetary tightening to anchor inflation expectations, which disproportionately elevates the short end of the curve and causes aggressive flattening, directly contradicting the term premium expansion theory.5  
This document deconstructs the quantitative evolution of the algorithmic strategy from a rudimentary binary regime-switching model into a complex, multi-factor framework incorporating Principal Component Analysis (PCA), dynamic risk-weighting, and macroprudential overrides. By dissecting the signal generation pipeline, execution mechanics, and systemic vulnerabilities, the analysis provides a comprehensive roadmap for optimizing regime-dependent fixed-income arbitrage in high-beta emerging markets. The evaluation emphasizes the necessity of decoupling from static developed-market heuristics and rigorously adapting algorithmic parameters to the idiosyncratic realities of Brazilian monetary policy.

## **The Macro-Financial Framework and the Brazilian Yield Curve Paradox**

To accurately contextualize the performance and architectural evolution of the trading strategy, it is imperative to establish the theoretical macroeconomic foundations of yield curve dynamics and contrast them with the empirical reality of the Brazilian financial system. The yield curve contains a vast repository of forward-looking information, reflecting aggregate market expectations regarding inflation, monetary policy, fiscal sustainability, and overall sovereign risk assessment.7

### **Theoretical Underpinnings of the Term Structure**

The literature governing yield curve dynamics relies on three primary theories to explain the term structure of interest rates. The Pure Expectations Theory asserts that, assuming no arbitrage conditions, long-term interest rates serve as unbiased predictors of future short-term interest rates. The Liquidity Preference Theory extends this framework by postulating that investors demand a risk premium—the term premium—to commit capital over longer horizons, thereby compensating for the inherent uncertainty of distant cash flows. Consequently, this theory dictates an inherently upward-sloping yield curve. Finally, the Market Segmentation Theory suggests that supply and demand dynamics within specific maturity buckets dictate yields, as distinct institutional participants maintain rigid duration mandates.7  
In advanced economies, the transmission mechanism of fiscal policy to the yield curve aligns closely with the Liquidity Preference Theory. Expansive fiscal policy or elevated uncertainty regarding sovereign debt sustainability typically steepens the yield curve. Market participants demand a higher term premium to hold long-dated government debt, fearing that unchecked fiscal expansion will eventually lead to debt monetization and long-term currency devaluation. Therefore, a purely quantitative algorithm operating in a developed market framework logically executes a bear steepener (shorting the long end relative to the short end) during regimes of fiscal distress.

### **The High-Uncertainty Flattening Paradox**

In the Brazilian macro-financial environment, the mechanical relationship between fiscal uncertainty and curve steepening is frequently inverted due to the highly reactive inflation-targeting framework managed by the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB).5 The fundamental vulnerability of the evaluated strategy lies in its reliance on executing a "bear steepener" during regimes of high fiscal uncertainty.  
When Brazilian fiscal parameters deteriorate, the market mechanism diverges significantly from developed counterparts. Elevated fiscal uncertainty immediately transmits into the foreign exchange market, precipitating a depreciation of the Brazilian Real (BRL). This currency depreciation, combined with fears of fiscal dominance, causes forward-looking inflation expectations to de-anchor.8 In response to de-anchoring expectations, the Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) of the BCB is structurally mandated to hike the primary policy rate (the Selic rate) aggressively.8  
This hawkish monetary reaction dominates the pricing of the yield curve. While the long-end of the curve may experience upward pressure due to a rising term premium, the short-end of the DI curve responds violently to the immediate anticipation of Selic rate hikes. Consequently, the 1-year segment of the curve rises at a substantially faster velocity than the 3-year or 5-year segments, leading to severe curve flattening or outright inversion.4  
This dynamic creates a severe "High-Uncertainty Paradox" for the algorithmic strategy. The quantitative signal correctly identifies a regime of fiscal distress, but the resulting position—a bear steepener—is fundamentally misaligned with the central bank's reaction function. The persistence of this flattening dynamic during sustained fiscal shocks, such as the protracted 2015-2016 political and economic crisis or the 2021-2022 policy normalization cycle, constitutes the primary vector of negative absolute returns across the strategy's evolutionary history.

## **Market Microstructure: Mechanics of the Brazilian DI FRA Market**

The execution layer of the algorithmic strategy operates exclusively within the Brazilian interest rate derivative market, specifically utilizing One-Day Interbank Deposit (DI) Futures traded on the B3 (Brasil, Bolsa, Balcão) exchange.2 Understanding the unique mathematical and structural properties of these instruments is crucial for evaluating the strategy's transaction cost models and duration-weighting mechanisms.

### **DI Futures Contract Specifications**

Unlike numerous global jurisdictions where the benchmark yield curve is derived directly from cash government bonds, the primary reference for the Brazilian term structure is the DI futures contract. This preference is driven by the immense liquidity of the derivative market, the efficiency of centralized exchange clearing, and the mitigation of pure sovereign default risk through stringent margin requirements.1  
The underlying asset of the DI1 contract is the capitalized daily Interbank Deposit (DI) rate, calculated and published by B3, accumulated between the trading date and the maturity date.3 The contract is uniquely quoted as an annualized percentage rate, compounded daily based on a 252-day business year, rather than a price index.2 The notional value of each contract is established at exactly BRL 100,000 at the maturity date. To determine the unit price (PU) of the contract on any given trading day, the final notional value is discounted back to the present using the traded interest rate.3  
The expiration dates of the DI1 futures contracts are strategically matched with the maturities of Brazilian domestic government bonds: Letras do Tesouro Nacional (LTNs), which are zero-coupon bonds maturing quarterly, and Notas do Tesouro Nacional – Série F (NTN-Fs), which are fixed-coupon bonds maturing annually in January.2 This alignment facilitates seamless arbitrage between the cash bond market and the derivative curve.

### **Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs) on the DI Curve**

The strategy explicitly targets the 1y1y versus 3y3y spread. In the context of the Brazilian market, these are implied Forward Rate Agreements constructed from the spot DI futures curve. To isolate the 1-year forward rate starting 1 year from today (1y1y), the algorithm mathematically extracts the rate that eliminates arbitrage between investing at the 2-year spot rate and investing at the 1-year spot rate followed by a 1-year forward rate.12  
Trading the 1y1y vs 3y3y spread requires executing four distinct DI futures contracts to synthesize the forward rates. A bear steepener necessitates taking a short position (paying fixed) on the 3y3y forward rate and a long position (receiving fixed) on the 1y1y forward rate. The daily settlement price of these contracts is calculated by B3 using a Volume-Weighted Average Price (VWAP) methodology during a specific ten-minute closing window to prevent market manipulation during periods of high volatility.3

### **Cross-Border Risk and the Local Curve**

While the strategy operates on the onshore DI curve, foreign institutional participation introduces complex cross-border risk dynamics that continuously influence the slope and level of the curve. These risks are bifurcated into transferability and convertibility risks.14 Transferability refers to the risk that the sovereign state may prevent the repatriation of onshore assets, regardless of their currency denomination. Convertibility refers to the risk that the state may prohibit the exchange of the domestic currency (BRL) into foreign currencies (USD).  
To compensate for these jurisdictional risks, onshore USD yields in Brazil command a premium over offshore USD yields. The difference between the onshore DI rate and the anticipated currency depreciation yields the "cupom cambial" (the onshore dollar rate).14 During periods of acute fiscal uncertainty, foreign capital flight exacerbates the widening of the cupom cambial, forcing the BCB to supply liquidity via foreign exchange swaps, which subsequently impacts the DI curve dynamics. A robust quantitative model must account for these international capital flows, as they frequently override pure domestic macroeconomic indicators.

## **Signal Engineering: Fiscal Uncertainty and Markov Regimes**

The foundational quantitative signal driving the trading framework relies on translating raw macroeconomic expectations into an actionable, mathematically rigorous regime indicator. The raw input variable is designated as std\_4y, representing the cross-sectional standard deviation of the 4-year-ahead primary surplus forecasts.15

### **The BCB Focus Survey**

The data is sourced directly from the BCB's Market Expectations System, commonly referred to as the Focus Survey. This system compiles forecasts from approximately 165 accredited financial institutions, banks, asset managers, and consultancies on a daily basis, releasing the aggregated statistics every Monday.15 The Focus survey tracks expectations for inflation indices (IPCA), GDP growth, exchange rates, the Selic target rate, and fiscal indicators.15  
The dispersion of these forecasts—measured by standard deviation—serves as an exceptionally pure proxy for systemic uncertainty. When consensus regarding the government's ability to maintain a primary surplus fractures, the dispersion widens, signaling elevated fiscal risk. Conversely, a tight clustering of forecasts indicates strong fiscal credibility and market consensus.

### **Structural Breaks and Data Non-Stationarity**

Analyzing the historical time series of std\_4y from 2001 to 2026 reveals severe non-stationarity, punctuated by a massive structural break occurring around 2014-2015. Prior to this period, the dispersion hovered consistently near a baseline of 0.05. However, during the severe economic contraction and fiscal deterioration that characterized the 2014-2016 period, the primary surplus targets were continuously revised downward, eventually culminating in a primary deficit of 0.85% of GDP in 2015\.16 Consequently, the forecast dispersion permanently shifted upward, establishing a new structural baseline above 0.45.  
This structural break presents a critical econometric challenge. A naive algorithm utilizing full-sample statistics would erroneously classify all pre-2015 data as "low uncertainty" and all post-2015 data as "high uncertainty," effectively destroying the model's ability to discern cyclical fluctuations within the new macro regime.

### **Markov-Switching Regime Architecture**

To navigate the non-stationary nature of the data and identify actionable trading environments, the strategy employs a Markov-switching regression model. First introduced into econometric modeling by Hamilton (1989), Markov-switching models allow the underlying data-generating process to vary across unobserved, discrete states or regimes.17 This non-linear approach is particularly suited for the Brazilian term structure, which is subject to abrupt shifts in monetary and fiscal policy frameworks.18  
The implementation utilizes a two-regime Markov-Switching Regression featuring switching variance, delineating a "Rising Uncertainty" state from a "Stable/Falling Uncertainty" state. However, directly applying the Markov model to the raw daily differences of the std\_4y metric proved mathematically unstable. The raw daily differences contain an extraordinarily low signal-to-noise ratio (approximately 0.002). Consequently, the maximum likelihood estimation forces the model to identify noise as regime transitions, resulting in a degenerative "barcode" effect where the algorithm erratically alternates regimes on a day-to-day basis.  
To resolve this instability, the signal engineering architecture applies a 20-day rolling mean to the daily differences of the std\_4y metric before subjecting the data to the Markov model. This smoothing technique effectively filters out idiosyncratic survey noise and transient volatility, allowing the model to capture persistent, low-frequency macroeconomic trends.  
The resulting regime classification is highly stable and operationally viable. The transition probability matrix yields a high degree of persistence for both regimes. The probability of remaining in a stable regime ($p\_{00}$) is calculated at approximately 0.978, indicating that once a calm fiscal environment is established, it tends to persist. The probability of remaining in an elevated uncertainty regime ($p\_{11}$) is estimated at 0.946. Converting these probabilities into expected durations indicates that stable regimes last an average of 45 trading days, while high-uncertainty regimes persist for an average of 18 trading days. This cadence aligns perfectly with the monthly rhythm of Brazilian fiscal data releases and Copom monetary policy meetings, validating the econometric integrity of the smoothed model.

## **Evolutionary Trajectory of the Algorithmic Strategy (Versions 1 to 4\)**

The development of the strategy demonstrates a progressive, iterative maturation from rigid, threshold-based heuristics to continuous, momentum-filtered position sizing. Analyzing the specific mathematical and conceptual failures of the initial four versions provides critical insight into the structural necessities of emerging market fixed-income arbitrage.

### **Version 1: The Binary Baseline**

The initial iteration (steepener\_strategy.py) implemented a strict binary classification framework based on the Markov probabilities. The core logic dictated that positions were established exclusively on formal fiscal release dates and held statically until the subsequent data release. The signal mechanism relied on a rigid threshold: if the probability of a "high volatility" regime exceeded 0.5, the algorithm executed a maximum-sized Bear Steepener ("Pay the Spread"). If the probability fell below 0.5, it executed a Bull Steepener ("Collect the Carry").  
The limitations of Version 1 were severe, resulting in a net negative performance of \-15.18 basis points. The static holding periods ignored all intra-month curve dynamics, leaving the portfolio exposed to sudden macroeconomic shocks occurring between fiscal releases. Furthermore, the binary threshold eradicated the nuanced informational content of the probability continuum; a 51% probability triggered the exact same position sizing as a 99% probability. Most critically, executing a bull steepener during low-uncertainty regimes proved directionally hazardous. In the Brazilian macro context, low fiscal uncertainty frequently coincides with robust economic growth, which ultimately prompts BCB tightening that flattens the curve.

### **Version 2: Probabilistic Scaling and Duration Mismatch**

To resolve the rigidity of the binary threshold, Version 2 (steepener\_strategy\_v2.py) transitioned to daily position management and introduced continuous probabilistic scaling. The position size was engineered to scale linearly based on the strength of the regime conviction. For example, as the probability of a high-uncertainty regime ascended from 50% to 80%, the steepener position scaled proportionally from 50% to 100% of maximum capacity.  
Despite the theoretical improvement in continuity, Version 2 suffered catastrophic drawdowns, generating the worst performance across the development cycle with a total P\&L of \-809.96 bps. The degradation was driven by a lethal combination of probability-scaled sizing and an entirely inadequate duration-matching model. The system relied on rudimentary weighting approximations (a 0.01 factor for the short leg and 0.03 for the long leg) rather than exact analytical duration metrics. Because the strategy retained the flawed logic of maintaining a bull steepener during low-volatility regimes, the inaccurate weighting caused the portfolio to accumulate massive, unhedged directional exposure to parallel shifts in the yield curve. When the BCB initiated aggressive hiking cycles, the portfolio suffered catastrophic directional losses that vastly overwhelmed any relative value slope gains.

### **Version 3: Percentile-Based Scaling and Look-Ahead Bias**

Recognizing the flaws in the Markov probability scaling, Version 3 (steepener\_strategy\_v3.py) pivoted the primary signal to the raw percentiles of the std\_4y distribution. The logic dictated that readings below the 25th percentile initiated a carry-optimized flattener bias, readings between the 25th and 75th percentiles triggered a linearly scaled steepener, and readings above the 75th percentile triggered a maximum-conviction bear steepener.  
Version 3 introduced the vital concept of utilizing a flattener to combat the BCB reaction function during specific regimes. However, the implementation utilized full-sample percentiles to define the thresholds. By calculating the 25th and 75th percentiles using the entire 2001-2026 dataset, the model allowed future data (specifically the extreme values of the post-2015 structural break) to influence trading decisions in the early 2000s. This irretrievably contaminated the backtest with look-ahead bias, rendering the performance metrics (-35.59 bps) invalid for predictive purposes.

### **Version 4: Momentum Integration**

Version 4 (steepener\_strategy\_v4.py) attempted to refine the execution by introducing a trend-following overlay designed to prevent the algorithm from fighting prevailing market momentum. The system computed a 20-day moving average of the 1y1y-3y3y spread. If the current spread was tightly compressed relative to the moving average during a high-uncertainty regime, the system increased the steepener size to capture mean reversion. Conversely, if the spread was already highly extended, the system reduced the position size to avoid chasing exhausted price action.  
While the theoretical inclusion of a momentum filter is sound practice, the specific parameterization in Version 4 was arbitrary, relying on hand-tuned multipliers without statistical justification. Combined with the lingering look-ahead bias inherent in the percentile calculations inherited from Version 3, the momentum overlay ultimately amplified losses, resulting in a net drag of \-183.87 bps over 301 trades.

## **Institutional Architecture and DV01 Neutrality (Version 5\)**

Version 5 (steepener\_strategy\_v5.py and base\_strategy.py) represents a systemic paradigm shift, transitioning the codebase from experimental heuristics into an institutional-grade, object-oriented framework. This iteration systematically eradicates look-ahead bias, implements exact mathematical duration matching, and introduces non-linear transaction cost modeling.

### **Elimination of Look-Ahead Bias via Expanding Windows**

To resolve the look-ahead bias that compromised Versions 3 and 4, the signal architecture abandons full-sample percentiles in favor of an expanding-window Z-score. The mathematical formulation is defined as:

$$Z\_t \= \\frac{\\text{std\\\_4y}\_t \- \\mu(\\text{std\\\_4y}\_{1..t})}{\\sigma(\\text{std\\\_4y}\_{1..t})}$$  
By anchoring the mean and standard deviation exclusively to the historical data available up to time $t$, the expanding Z-score ensures strict out-of-sample fidelity.  
Version 5 synthesizes this expanding Z-score with the Markov probability to create a confidence-weighted signal matrix. The Z-score serves as the primary magnitude indicator, while the Markov probability acts as a structural confirmation filter. A Z-score above 0.5 confirmed by a Markov probability above 0.5 triggers a heavily sized bear steepener. If the signals conflict (e.g., high Z-score but low Markov probability), the position size is significantly dampened. Crucially, a Z-score below \-0.5 confirmed by a low Markov probability triggers a genuine flattener position (allowing negative sizing up to \-0.5), acknowledging the necessity of fading the steepener during low-risk regimes.

### **DV01-Neutral Position Sizing**

The absolute central requirement of a relative value yield curve strategy is the isolation of slope dynamics from parallel shifts in the broader interest rate environment.20 If a portfolio holds equal nominal quantities of a 1-year instrument and a 3-year instrument, a parallel upward shift of 100 basis points across the entire curve will generate disproportionately massive losses on the 3-year leg due to its higher duration.22 To eliminate this directional bias, the strategy must be weighted on a Dollar Value of a Basis Point (DV01) neutral basis.23  
The DV01 is defined mathematically as the absolute change in the dollar value of a fixed-income instrument for a one basis point decline in the underlying yield.23 Version 5 achieves exact neutrality by equating the risk across both legs of the FRA:

$$N\_{1y} \\times DV01\_{1y} \= N\_{3y} \\times DV01\_{3y} \= R$$  
Where $N$ represents the notional amount, and $R$ represents the target risk allocation per leg. Utilizing the analytical DV01 values specific to the Brazilian DI curve—0.98 for the 1y1y leg and 2.72 for the 3y3y leg—the system constructs a portfolio immune to parallel curve shifts.20 The resulting daily Profit and Loss (P\&L) function is isolated purely to the differential movement between the two tenors:

$$\\text{Curve P\\\&L} \= \\text{Position Size} \\times R \\times (\\Delta 3y3y \- \\Delta 1y1y)$$  
This rigorous mathematical weighting ensures that the algorithm functions as a true slope arbitrage, generating returns based on the relative compression or expansion of the term premium, entirely independent of the absolute level of the BCB's Selic rate.

### **Non-Linear Market Impact and Stop-Loss Mechanics**

To simulate the execution realities of the B3 exchange, Version 5 abandons static cost assumptions in favor of a quadratic market impact model. The execution cost is formulated as:

$$\\text{Cost} \= \\text{Base Spread} \+ \\text{Impact Factor} \\times \\text{Size}^2$$  
This non-linear formulation correctly penalizes large, aggressive orders. During high-uncertainty regimes requiring immediate liquidity, the system applies a $2\\times$ multiplier to the impact factor. During stable regimes, the system simulates passive limit order placement, recovering a portion of the base spread to mimic the collection of the bid-ask spread and localized roll-down.  
Furthermore, Version 5 implements a rigid 50 bps trailing stop-loss to truncate left-tail risk. If the cumulative drawdown from the local high-water mark exceeds 50 bps, the system immediately flattens all risk. However, quantitative analysis of the backtest reveals a severe flaw in the stop-loss reset condition. The system mandates that the Z-score must return to a neutral threshold ($|Z| \< 0.5$) before risk can be reinstated. During the protracted Brazilian fiscal crisis of 2016, the std\_4y metric remained structurally elevated for over a year. The stop-loss was triggered early in the cycle, and the restrictive reset condition kept the strategy flat for over 100 consecutive trading days. Consequently, the algorithm completely missed the massive curve steepening (+154 bps) that occurred during the political transition and subsequent reform announcements in late 2016\.  
Despite this operational flaw, the structural improvements in Version 5 vastly reduced the catastrophic drawdowns seen in earlier versions, concluding with a net P\&L of \-24.11 bps across 518 trades, and a win rate of 37.6%.

## **Macroprudential Filtering via Dynamic PCA (Version 6\)**

While Version 5 achieved structural and mathematical rigor, it remained fundamentally vulnerable to the High-Uncertainty Paradox. The strategy continued to execute bear steepeners during fiscal crises, only to suffer losses as the BCB aggressively hiked the Selic rate, flattening the curve. To directly combat the central bank's reaction function, Version 6 (steepener\_strategy\_v6.py) integrates a sophisticated macroeconomic risk filter utilizing Principal Component Analysis (PCA).

### **Dimensionality Reduction and Macroeconomic Stress**

PCA is widely employed in advanced fixed-income literature to reduce the dimensionality of the yield curve into latent factors—typically interpreted as level, slope, and curvature.26 Version 6 repurposes this mathematical technique to construct a dynamic, multi-factor indicator of macroeconomic hostility.  
The algorithm expands the data ingestion pipeline beyond isolated fiscal uncertainty. It integrates the 12-month forward expectations for the IPCA (Brazil's broad consumer price index) and the 1-year forward expectations for the Selic rate, tracking both the median and standard deviation of these metrics via the Focus survey.6 By monitoring the exact parameters that dictate the BCB's inflation-targeting mandate, the algorithm effectively models the central bank's probability of intervention.  
The system executes a Dynamic PCA over a rolling 252-day window (equivalent to one trading year) across this matrix of fiscal, inflation, and monetary expectations. Through eigen decomposition of the covariance matrix, the algorithm extracts the first principal component (PC1). PC1 mathematically represents the vector of maximum variance across all the input variables, effectively functioning as a composite index of "Macroeconomic Stress".11

### **The Dynamic Inflation Filter**

To translate the continuous PC1 metric into an actionable trading rule, the system subjects the extracted component to an expanding-window percentile classification. This establishes rolling 30th and 70th percentile thresholds, ensuring the classification remains completely free of look-ahead bias while adapting to changing macroeconomic baselines. These thresholds segment the environment into Low, Medium, and High inflation expectation regimes.  
The implementation of the risk override is absolute and draconian: if the PC1 indicates that the economy has entered the "High" inflation expectation regime (breaching the 70th percentile), or if the Markov probability of a fiscal crisis simultaneously exceeds 0.60, the strategy triggers a hard block and maintains a zero net position.  
This macroprudential filter represents the state-of-the-art iteration of the framework. By blocking trades when inflation expectations are at dynamic highs, the strategy explicitly sidesteps the periods where the BCB is guaranteed to hike the Selic rate and flatten the curve.4 It ensures that the algorithm only executes bear steepeners when fiscal uncertainty is rising, but inflation and monetary policy expectations remain firmly anchored—the precise and rare macroeconomic alignment required for the classic term premium expansion theory to hold true in an emerging market context.

## **Critical Systemic Vulnerabilities**

Despite the mathematical sophistication of Version 6, the system retains several embedded assumptions and vulnerabilities that threaten its viability in live trading environments. A rigorous quantitative evaluation highlights severe deficiencies in the execution parameters, the static nature of the duration matching, and the absence of funding cost differentials.

### **Static DV01 Ratios and Unhedged Convexity**

The implementation of DV01 neutrality in Versions 5 and 6 relies on static values (0.98 for the 1y1y and 2.72 for the 3y3y). While this ratio effectively neutralizes parallel shifts at a single, theoretical point in time, it completely ignores the dynamic, non-linear nature of fixed-income duration.  
As market yields fluctuate, the mathematical derivatives governing the price-yield relationship shift continuously.33 Furthermore, matching first-derivative DV01s does not immunize the portfolio against second-derivative convexity risk.22 Convexity measures the rate of change of duration; it defines the non-linear curvature of the bond pricing function. The 3y3y leg, possessing a significantly longer time to maturity, exhibits a distinctly higher convexity profile than the 1y1y leg.  
During periods of extreme macroeconomic volatility—precisely the tail-risk regimes the strategy is engineered to target—massive yield dislocations will cause the static DV01 hedge ratio to deviate substantially from reality. As yields spike, the duration of the long leg falls faster than the duration of the short leg due to its higher convexity. This dynamic re-introduces massive directional risk into the purportedly neutral portfolio, leading to unexpected "convexity bleed".22 Maintaining true neutrality requires continuous, intraday algorithmic recalibration of the hedge ratios based on prevailing market yield levels.

### **Asymmetric Funding Costs and Negative Carry Traps**

The P\&L architecture across the entire codebase rigorously evaluates curve spread dynamics and approximated transaction costs, but it critically omits the funding differential between the short and long legs of the FRA. Constructing a bear steepener requires synthetically paying fixed on the long end and receiving fixed on the short end.  
In a macroeconomic regime where the spot Selic rate is aggressively elevated by the BCB to combat inflation, the cost of financing the short-end position frequently exceeds the yield accrued on the long end.35 This creates a scenario of deep negative carry. If the yield curve spread remains stagnant, the portfolio bleeds capital daily simply to maintain the position. A robust quantitative model must integrate the exact financing spread (the spread between the overnight DI rate and the Selic rate) and deduct this carrying cost from the daily P\&L attribution to reflect the true, punitive economics of the trade during monetary tightening cycles.

### **The Illusion of Continuous Pricing and Liquidity Scalars**

The non-linear slippage model assumes that transaction costs scale purely as a mathematical function of nominal size. However, this ignores the temporal realities of the B3 exchange order books. Liquidity in the Brazilian DI market is highly concentrated in specific front-month contracts and is heavily influenced by the macroeconomic calendar.1  
During severe fiscal shocks or political crises, the bid-ask spread on the 3y3y FRA can widen exponentially, and order book depth can evaporate entirely. A volumetric scalar must be introduced to discount the algorithmic position size proportionally to the trailing 5-day volume of the underlying contracts. Executing maximum-notional steepeners in illiquid, stressed markets fundamentally breaks the theoretical assumptions of the slippage model and virtually guarantees severe execution slippage.

## **Strategic Enhancements and Optimization Roadmap**

To elevate the strategy to profitability and ensure structural resilience against the idiosyncratic behaviors of the Brazilian fixed-income market, a series of advanced quantitative enhancements must be integrated into the architecture.

### **1\. Inversion of the High-Uncertainty Signal**

The most consequential and immediately actionable enhancement involves redefining the strategy's response to extreme tail events. Currently, the PCA filter in Version 6 acts as a circuit breaker, blocking trades during extreme inflationary pressure. However, a more accretive algorithmic approach would be to actively invert the signal.  
When the expanding Z-score of fiscal uncertainty exceeds a critical standard deviation threshold (e.g., $Z \> 1.5$) and is simultaneously confirmed by a "High" PCA inflation expectation score, the probability of aggressive BCB intervention approaches absolute certainty.6 Rather than retreating to cash, the strategy should systematically execute a maximum-conviction flattener (short the 1y1y, long the 3y3y). By aligning the algorithm with the central bank's reaction function during crises, the framework transforms its primary source of historical drawdown into its most potent alpha generator, profiting directly from the structural flattening of the Brazilian curve.

### **2\. Integration of Cross-Asset Volatility Confirmation**

Emerging market yield curves do not function in a vacuum; they are heavily influenced by the gravitational pull of global financial conditions and international sovereign risk pricing.37 Relying exclusively on the domestic Focus survey for signal generation exposes the model to localized forecasting lags and optimistic domestic bias.  
The integration of high-frequency offshore risk proxies is essential. Utilizing the 5-year Brazilian Credit Default Swap (CDS) spread and the implied volatility of the USD/BRL exchange rate provides real-time, tradable confirmation of fiscal distress.11 If the domestic std\_4y metric signals a steepening environment, but the sovereign CDS curve is compressing and FX volatility is subdued, the system should interpret the divergence as a false positive and dampen the position size. Conversely, a simultaneous, correlated spike across local uncertainty metrics and offshore risk pricing provides maximum conviction for the signal.

### **3\. Transition to Volatility-Targeted Position Sizing**

The reliance on absolute Z-scores and percentile mappings for position sizing exposes the portfolio to massive swings in daily P\&L variance. A superior institutional approach is the implementation of strict volatility targeting.  
Under this framework, the algorithm continuously computes the 60-day trailing standard deviation of the daily P\&L of a baseline 1-unit position. The system then defines a strict target daily volatility threshold (e.g., 1 basis point of total portfolio risk). The notional position size is dynamically scaled inversely to the trailing volatility:

$$\\text{Position Size}\_t \= \\text{Base Size} \\times \\left( \\frac{\\text{Target Volatility}}{\\text{Trailing Volatility}\_{t-1}} \\right)$$  
This mechanism automatically deleverages the portfolio during hostile, high-variance market regimes and intelligently increases exposure during calm, trending periods, ensuring a symmetric, predictable risk profile independent of the broader macroeconomic environment.

### **4\. Overhaul of the Stop-Loss Architecture**

The rigid reset condition of the trailing stop-loss (requiring $|Z| \< 0.5$) must be replaced with a time-decaying cooldown mechanism to prevent the "100-day flatline" scenario observed in 2016\. When the 50 bps trailing stop is triggered, the system should enforce a hard blackout period of 20 trading days. Following the blackout, the system should employ a linear interpolation function to gradually reinstate allowable risk over the subsequent month. This approach protects capital during acute market dislocations while ensuring the strategy can participate in the inevitable post-crisis market normalizations.  
Furthermore, the static 50 bps threshold should be converted into a dynamic parameter indexed to the prevailing Average True Range (ATR) of the 1y1y-3y3y spread. A stop-loss calibrated to $2 \\times \\text{ATR}$ ensures that the portfolio is not whipsawed out of profitable trades during naturally volatile regimes, while maintaining tight leashes during periods of low volatility.

### **5\. Implementation of Walk-Forward Hyperparameter Optimization**

The parameters dictating the Z-score thresholds, Markov confidence scaling, and PCA lookback windows across Versions 5 and 6 are currently hardcoded. This static configuration increases the probability of curve-fitting to specific historical epochs within the backtest.  
A rigorous Walk-Forward Optimization (WFO) routine is mandatory for deployment. The system should iteratively train the parameters using cross-validation on a rolling 3-year historical window and validate the out-of-sample performance on the subsequent 1-year block. This continuous algorithmic recalibration ensures that the signal thresholds adapt naturally to long-term structural evolutions in the Brazilian economy, mitigating the permanent anchoring effect caused by the 2015 fiscal data break and ensuring true robustness across decades of market data.

## **Conclusion**

The architectural evolution of the fiscal uncertainty steepener strategy demonstrates a profound maturation in quantitative fixed-income design. The transition from the rigid binary classifications of Version 1 to the institutional-grade, exact-duration-matched architecture of Version 5 reflects an acute understanding of portfolio risk neutralization. Furthermore, the integration of the dynamic PCA macroprudential filter in Version 6 represents an elegant mathematical solution to the extreme complexities of modeling the Central Bank of Brazil's reaction function.  
However, the consistent realization of negative absolute returns across the backtests underscores the unique, frequently counter-intuitive challenges of trading the Brazilian DI curve. The mechanical assumption that fiscal distress equates to curve steepening is a heuristic of advanced economies that violently fractures under the reality of emerging market inflation dynamics and aggressive monetary intervention.  
The path to robust, scalable alpha generation requires transcending the original term premium thesis. By actively inverting the algorithmic signal during tail-risk events to capture structural flattening, implementing dynamic DV01 recalibration to eliminate convexity bleed, integrating punitive funding costs into the P\&L model, and enforcing strict volatility-targeted position sizing, the framework can be optimized to exploit the unique, Selic-driven mechanics of the Brazilian yield curve. The advanced mathematical foundation established in Version 6 provides the optimal scaffolding to support these critical final refinements, positioning the strategy for viable institutional deployment.
