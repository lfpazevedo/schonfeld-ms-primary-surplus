### 2. Markov Regime Bug — Two Fixes

**Problem 1: Structural break in raw std_4y**
- Raw `std_4y` level has a bimodal distribution (~0.05 pre-2015 → ~0.45+ post-2015)
- Markov model assigned ~99% of observations to a single regime
- **Fix**: Use differenced series (Δstd_4y) instead of levels

**Problem 2: Daily diffs too noisy ("barcode" pattern)**
- Daily diff SNR ≈ 0.002 (mean=0.00007, std=0.029)
- Model flipped regimes on every single day (persistence p00=0.55, p11=0.52)
- **Fix**: Use **20-day rolling mean of daily diffs** as Markov input

| Metric | Raw daily diffs | 20d smoothed diffs |
|---|---|---|
| Persistence p00, p11 | 0.55, 0.52 | **0.978, 0.946** |
| Avg regime duration | ~2 days | **~45 / ~18 days** |
| Regime split | 52% / 48% | **72% / 28%** |
| V5 Total P&L | -87.36 bps | **-24.11 bps** |

## Open Issues for Tomorrow

### Issue 1: High Uncertainty Regime Loses Money

The bear steepener signal **works in low/medium uncertainty** (+68.6 / +3.1 bps) but **bleeds during high uncertainty** (-95.8 bps, 19.8% win rate). This is the dominant source of losses.

**Possible explanation**: During high fiscal uncertainty in Brazil, the BCB hikes Selic → short end rises faster → curve **flattens**, not steepens. The bear steepener is the wrong trade in crises.

**Options to consider**:
1. **Invert signal during high uncertainty** — go flattener instead of steepener when Z-score is high
2. **Sit out high uncertainty** — only trade in low/medium regimes (would turn +71.7 bps with 2000 fewer high-uncertainty days)
3. **Reduce sizing** during high uncertainty instead of eliminating

### Issue 2: Stop-Loss Keeps Strategy Flat for Months

**Case study: 2016 Dilma impeachment**

```
Jan 29 – Feb 5:   Bear steepener +90%, lost -54.76 bps cumulative
Feb 10:           Stop-loss triggered (trailing drawdown > 50 bps)
Feb 10 → Jul 1:   FLAT for 100 consecutive days
                   Z-score stayed 1.65–3.98, never reset below 0.5
```

The stop-loss correctly protected from further losses on the original trade, but the reset condition (`|z| < 0.5`) is too strict during a sustained regime shift. The strategy missed the entire Temer takeover rally (May 2016 massive steepening).

**Options to consider**:
1. **Time-based cooldown** (e.g., 20 days flat then re-enter) instead of Z-score-based reset
2. **Remove stop-loss entirely** and let regime/Z-score sizing manage risk naturally
3. **Hybrid**: stop-loss with time decay — after N days flat, gradually re-enter

### Issue 3: Economic Signal Question

On 2016-01-29, `std_4y` was at ~0.5 (not extreme on absolute level) but Z-score was 0.90 (elevated relative to expanding history). The strategy went full bear steepener (+90%) and lost 39 bps in one day.

**Question**: Is the expanding Z-score the right signal? It's anchored to the entire history back to 2002, which includes the ~0.2–0.3 pre-crisis period. By Jan 2016, even a "moderate" std_4y of 0.5 looks high relative to very early data. Consider:
- Using a **rolling window Z-score** (e.g., 252-day) instead of expanding
- Using **percentile rank** over a fixed window as the signal
